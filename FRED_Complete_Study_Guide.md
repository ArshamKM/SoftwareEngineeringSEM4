# Section 1: Design & The Design Process

## What is Design?

Design is a concept of or proposal for an object, a process, or a system that concerns not only the beautiful part of the product but also its **functionality** and how they collaborate with each other.

The satisfaction of the design is fulfilled when the **experience of the user** while interacting with the system is satisfiable.

> **Don Norman's General Design Principle:** Good design is a lot harder to notice than poor design, in part because good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible, serving us without drawing attention to itself. Bad design, on the other hand, **screams**.

Don Norman also noted that good observation skills + good design principles benefit us all — because we are all designers in the sense that all of us deliberately design our lives, our rooms, and the way we do things.

## The Design Process (5 Steps)

The design process moves from abstract idea to detailed solution. Each step answers a key question:

| Step | What it is & key question it answers |
|---|---|
| **1. Design Brief** | Describes the target design solution. Who is it for? What are its features and personality? What activities should it enable or encourage? |
| **2. Design Principles** | Defines what the experience of using the product should feel like to a user. Sets the values and direction for all design decisions. |
| **3. Sketching** | Explores different forms the product design could take. Quick, rough drawings of ideas — no commitment yet. |
| **4. Sketchboards** | Shows what the overall system or product might look like and what range of ideas are possible at each point in the process. |
| **5. Task Flows / Wireframes** | Task flows show how the experience unfolds over time. Wireframes show how the product will look and function in detail — schematic blueprints. |

> **The Squiggle (Damien Newman):** Conveys the messiness of the design process — starts chaotic with many ideas and uncertainty, gradually narrows to clarity and a focused solution.

## Relevant Design Areas

- **Interaction Design:** Focuses on how people interact with technology. Goal: enhance understanding of what can be done, what is happening, and what has just occurred. Draws upon positive, enjoyable experience.
- **Experience Design:** The practice of designing products, processes, services, events, and environments with focus on the quality and enjoyment of the total experience.

## Introduction to UI/UX

- **Why it matters:** The complexity of modern devices confuses and sometimes frightens people. Design is concerned with how things work, how they are controlled, and the nature of interaction between people and technology.
- **The product rule:** Products must satisfy not only the requirements of engineering, manufacturing, and ergonomics — attention must also be paid to the entire experience: the aesthetics of form and quality of interaction.

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **UI (User Interface)** | The visual and interactive elements users see and manipulate — buttons, menus, layout, typography, colour. |
| **UX (User Experience)** | The overall experience a user has while interacting with a product, system, or service. Encompasses perceptions, emotions, and responses. Focuses on creating seamless, enjoyable, meaningful experiences — not just the interface. |

# Section 2: Fundamental Principles of Interaction
> ⚠️ **EXAM CRITICAL — Know all 6 definitions AND examples**

## 1. Affordances
Refers to the relationship between the product and a person. It is a relationship between the properties of an object and the capabilities of the agent that determines just how the object could possibly be used.

- A chair affords support and therefore affords **sitting**.
- A glass affords seeing through but **not** the passage of air or most objects.
- **Anti-affordance:** The prevention of interaction — when a design prevents a possible action (e.g. a wall affords no passage). Must be discoverable and perceivable.

## 2. Signifiers
Communicates where the action should take place — hints and clues on where to find what, some sign of what it is for, what is happening, and where alternative actions are.

- **Example:** Door hardware can signal to push or pull. Without a sign, you guess.

> **KEY RULE:** In design, signifiers are **MORE IMPORTANT** than affordances — because they communicate **how** to use the design. Affordances = possible actions. Signifiers = how to perform them.

## 3. Constraints
Constraints make people remember things more easily. Joint constraints can cause a dramatic reduction in the number of possible candidates, sometimes reducing a large set to a single choice.

| Type | Definition & Example |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Physical limitations prevent wrong actions. Example: a large peg cannot fit into a small hole. |
| **Cultural** | Social/cultural conventions restrict actions. Example: red = danger/stop in most cultures. |
| **Semantic** | Meaningful context limits actions. Example: it is meaningful to have a windshield in front of the rider's face for protection. |
| **Logical** | Logical reasoning limits options to one sensible choice. Example: if only one screw is left and one hole, the screw must go there. |

## 4. Mappings
The relationship between controls and their actions. In principle, it should be easy to remember the relationship between the controllers and their action.

- **Example:** A steering wheel — turn right and the car wheels turn right. Natural mapping. You cannot make a car where wheels turn left when you turn right — that would violate the expected mapping.
- **Natural mapping:** When the relationship between control and result follows physical or logical intuition. Best mappings require no labels or instructions.

## 5. Feedback
Feedback is communicating the results of an action by the user. Feedback should be **IMMEDIATE** — even a delay of a tenth of a second can be disconcerting.

If the delay is too long, people often give up and go off to do other activities. This is annoying to people but also wasteful when the system spends time satisfying a request only to find the user is no longer there.

## 6. Conceptual Model
A conceptual model is an explanation, usually highly simplified, of how something works. It does not have to be complete or even accurate as long as it is **useful**.

- **Example:** Files, folders, and icons on a computer screen help people create a conceptual model of documents inside the computer — even though the computer does not actually contain physical folders.
- Also defined as: the agreement we make upon certain things — shared understanding of how a system works.

# Section 3: Human-Centred Design (HCD)

## What is HCD?

People and technology are getting further apart every time a new complexity layer is added to our lives. Most of the time humans are blamed ("human error") while the design actually failed to achieve its purpose — so badly designed that a mistake was inevitable.

> **HCD DEFINITION:** This approach puts human needs, capabilities, and behaviour **FIRST**, then designs to accommodate those needs, capabilities, and ways of behaving.

HCD ensures that:
- People's needs are met
- The resulting product is understandable and usable
- It accomplishes the desired tasks and concerns

HCD is a **problem-solving approach** that focuses on users, collaboration, and iterative prototyping.

## HCD Core Rules

> **NEVER solve the problem you are asked to solve!** The secret to success is to understand what the **real problem** is.

We start by:
- Questioning the problem given
- Expanding the scope of the problem — **diverging** to examine all fundamental issues
- Defining the problem statement(s)

In the solution phase, we:
- **Expand** the space of possible solutions (diverge)
- **Converge** upon a proposed solution

## Good Design Requires (HCD Communication Rules)
- Good communication — especially from machine to human — indicating what actions are possible
- Communicating what is happening and what is about to happen
- Communication is especially important when things **go wrong**

## HCD Principles (All 10 — Know Every One)

| Principle | What it means |
|---|---|
| **User Involvement** | Users should be involved from requirements gathering to testing and evaluation. |
| **Empathy** | Understand the user and their needs deeply. |
| **Holistic Approach** | Consider not just the user interface but the entire user journey. |
| **Iterative Design** | Designs are tested and refined with user feedback. Ongoing cycle. |
| **Usability** | Product should be easy to learn, efficient to use, and error-tolerant. |
| **Accessibility** | Designers should consider a wide range of users and their needs. |
| **Consistency** | A consistent UI and experience reduces user confusion and learning curve. |
| **Feedback and Communication** | Users should receive clear feedback about their interactions with the system. |
| **Flexibility and Efficiency** | Provide flexibility for users, allowing them to accomplish tasks efficiently. |
| **Prototyping and Testing** | Allows designers to gather feedback early. Testing identifies issues and opportunities for improvement. |
| **User-Centred Research** | Gather data about user behaviour and preferences — this research informs design decisions. |
| **Iterative Improvement** | HCD is an ongoing process — continually refine based on what is learned. |

## The Iterative Cycle of HCD — 4 Core Stages

| Stage | What happens |
|---|---|
| **1. Observation (Empathy & Define)** | Initial research to understand the nature of the problem. Study personas closely (applied ethnography). Observe stakeholders' activities, interests, motives and true needs. The problem definition will come from deep understanding of people's goals. |
| **2. Idea Generation (Ideate)** | Three rules: (1) Generate numerous ideas — do not fixate on 1-2 early on. (2) Be creative without regard for constraints — avoid criticising ideas, your own or others'. (3) Question everything — what's obvious might not be. |
| **3. Prototyping** | Build a quick prototype or mock-up of each potential solution. Focus on MVPs. Use techniques such as Wizard of Oz. Goal: find out which direction to continue and gain feedback. |
| **4. Testing** | Gather a small group (minimum 2 people) from the target population. Have someone observe. Record the test if possible. Collect input after testing. Goal: ensure the problem is well understood. |

After testing, **repeat all four stages**. After the first few iterations, start converging upon a single solution.

## Activity-Centred vs Human-Centred Design

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Activity-Centred** | Focuses on the task or activity (e.g. 'go shopping'). An activity is a collected set of tasks performed together toward a common high-level goal (e.g. 'drive to market', 'find a basket', 'use shopping list'). |
| **Human-Centred** | Focuses on the PERSON doing the activity — their emotions, frustrations, goals, and context. Asks: who is this person and what do they truly need? |

## Linear (Waterfall) vs Iterative (Agile) Design

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| **Linear / Waterfall** | Progress in a single direction. Once decisions are made it is difficult or impossible to go back. Big 1.0 launch. Works in isolation then hands off to team. Hopes customers love the product after it is built. |
| **Iterative / Agile** | Process is circular. Continual refinement and change. Encourages backtracking and rethinking early decisions. Plans for small incremental pre-releases (MVPs). Collaborates continuously. Insists on experiments to test key features along the way. |

# Section 4: UX Research

## Why Do UX Research?
- To create a product that is truly **relevant** to users
- To create a product that is **easy and pleasurable** to use *(Steve Jobs: "If the user is having a problem, it is our problem.")*
- To have the **ROI** of user experience design validated
- To measure impact on business, conversion rates, and user satisfaction

UX research is not done once — it is used in **ALL phases** of the HCD cycle.

## The UX Research Process (5 Steps)

| Step | Description |
|---|---|
| **1. Define** | Understanding the problem. Aligning with business and user goals. |
| **2. Plan** | Choosing the right research method for the question. |
| **3. Conduct** | Gathering insights from users. |
| **4. Analyze** | Synthesizing findings into actionable insights. |
| **5. Apply** | Implementing research findings into design decisions. |

## Research Principles (Know All 7)
- **Design cannot be fully objective:** Research helps inform design and remove some bias, but it is still conducted by a human who cannot fully remove their own bias.
- **Do just enough research:** Avoid over-researching everything OR doing no research at all.
- **Map the ecosystem:** Understand the business and all its products/services to gain a holistic view.
- **Not everything that counts can be counted:** Qualitative research where results cannot be counted still matters enormously.
- **Don't base personas on assumptions:** Personas are a discovery tool — they remind the team they are designing for actual people.
- **Keep your enemies close:** Choose 4-7 competitor products to compare. Identify what makes each unique, industry standards, shared features, and innovation space.
- **Learn from bad examples:** Compare executing the same action on your project vs competitors.
- **Uncover consensus and ambiguity:** Collect what all people agree upon AND what all people hate.

## Types of UX Research

| Type | Description, Methods & Key Points |
|---|---|
| **Quantitative (What?)** | Focuses on numerical data, statistical patterns. Methods: Surveys, Google Analytics. Answers: **WHAT** happens. |
| **Qualitative (Why?)** | Focuses on human emotions, behaviours, motivations. Methods: Focus groups, User Interviews, Field Studies. Answers: **WHY** it happens. |
| **Attitudinal (What they say)** | Measures user perceptions, opinions, attitudes. Methods: Surveys, Focus groups, Card sorting. Can be either Quantitative or Qualitative. |
| **Behavioral (What they do)** | Observes actual user behaviour. Methods: Usability testing, Eye-tracking, Clickstream analysis. Can be either Quantitative or Qualitative. |
| **Generative (Discover)** | Used at EARLY stages to explore problems. Usually Qualitative & Attitudinal. Methods: Ethnographic studies, User interviews, Focus groups. |
| **Evaluative (Validate)** | Conducted at LATER stages to validate ideas. Often Quantitative & Behavioral. Methods: Usability testing, A/B testing, Heuristic evaluation. |

> **EXAM TIP:** Always trust **Behavioral over Attitudinal**. What users **DO** is more reliable than what they **SAY**.

## Analysis vs Synthesis

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Analysis** | Breaking findings down into facts. Provides a set of facts from your research data. |
| **Synthesis** | Bringing ALL findings together into insights, conclusions, and actionable recommendations. Makes those facts meaningful. Always revisit the original research goals during synthesis. |

## Key Research Methods & Tools

**Affinity Mapping:**
1. Write all data points on post-its
2. Stick all post-its on a wall/whiteboard
3. Move post-its into groups based on common themes
4. Label each group
→ Produces themes and insights from raw data

**Empathy Map:** A quick way to illustrate user attitudes and behaviours. Categorises knowledge into: what the user **Says**, **Thinks**, **Does**, and **Feels**. Should act as a source of truth throughout a project. Revise as you do more research.

## Usability Testing

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Roles** | User (U) — performs the task. Moderator/Recorder (M/R) — observes and records. |
| **Rule** | **Observe, Don't Interpret** — capture what the user says, does, and feels without jumping to conclusions yet. |
| **M/R Rules** | Do NOT help. Ask the user to 'think aloud'. Capture what they say. Capture what they do. Capture visible confusion or hesitation. NO interpretation yet. |
| **What to Capture** | Time \| User Says \| User Does \| Emotion |
| **Debrief Questions** | Where did the user hesitate? What surprised you? What expectation was violated? Which heuristic was broken? |

## Bias in UX Research

| Bias Type | Description & How to Mitigate |
|---|---|
| **Sampling Bias** | Selected participants do not represent the target user population. Mitigation: Ensure diverse and representative participant recruitment. |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Researchers seek or give more weight to information that confirms their preconceptions. Mitigation: Adopt a neutral stance and consider alternative perspectives during analysis. |
| **Social Desirability Bias** | Participants provide responses they think are more socially acceptable. Mitigation: Create a comfortable environment assuring participants that honest feedback is valued and confidential. |

## GDPR and UX Research

| GDPR Requirement | What it means for UX Research |
|---|---|
| **Informed Consent** | Researchers must obtain EXPLICIT consent from participants before collecting personal data. Clearly explain the purpose, methods, and usage of the data. |
| **Data Minimization** | Only collect data that is directly relevant and necessary for the research purpose. Avoid gathering excessive or unrelated personal information. |
| **Right to Access and Erasure** | Participants have the right to access their data and request its deletion. Implement processes to accommodate such requests promptly. |
| **Data Security** | Ensure personal data is stored securely to prevent unauthorized access. Use encryption and other security measures. |

# Section 5: Accessibility & Inclusive Design

## What is Accessibility?

Web accessibility means that websites, tools, and technologies are designed so that **people with disabilities can use them**.

- An estimated **1.3 billion people — 1 in 6 people worldwide** — experience significant disability (WHO, 2022).
- 75% of Americans with disabilities report using the internet on a daily basis.

> **Tim Berners-Lee (inventor of the World Wide Web):** "The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect."

## Benefits of Accessible Design
- **Helps all users:** Video captions help people in noisy environments. High-contrast text helps people in bright sunlight.
- **Improved SEO:** Semantic HTML and alt text improve search engine ranking.
- **Increased reach:** Access the 1 in 6 people who have disabilities.
- **Enhanced brand image:** Demonstrates social responsibility.
- **Improved UX overall:** Accessible design is simply better design.

## Disability Types & Design Responses

| Type | Examples & Design Responses |
|---|---|
| **Mobility impairments** | Cannot use a mouse. Design response: full keyboard navigation, large touch targets. |
| **Vision impairments** | Blind or low vision. Design response: alt text, screen reader support, high contrast, text-to-speech. |
| **Hearing impairments** | Deaf or hard of hearing. Design response: captions, transcripts, visual notifications, sign language. |
| **Learning difficulties** | Dyslexia, cognitive impairments. Design response: easy-to-read text, clear structure, consistent layouts, summaries. |

## Accessibility Features & Tools
- **Audio descriptions** — describe important visual details in a video
- **Captions** — verbatim text for any recording
- **Transcripts** — text containing verbatim recording of speech and descriptions of important auditory or visual information
- **Text-to-speech** — converts written text to spoken audio
- **Screen magnification** — enlarge or reduce text size and images
- **Assistive Technologies:** Screen readers, Screen magnifiers, Voice recognition software
- **Adaptive Strategies:** Increasing text size, reducing mouse speed, turning on captions

## WCAG 2.0 — Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

Developed by **W3C** via its **Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)**.
- WCAG 2.0 published December 2008 | WCAG 2.1 — June 2018 | WCAG 2.2 — October 2023
- **4 principles, 13 guidelines**
- Three compliance levels: **A** (lowest acceptable), **AA** (standard — most organisations target this), **AAA** (most strict)

### POUR Principles

| Principle | Detailed requirements | Key question |
|---|---|---|
| **P — Perceivable** | Provide text alternatives for non-text content. Provide captions for multimedia. Create content that can be presented in different ways without losing meaning. | Can the content be consumed in different ways? |
| **O — Operable** | Make all functionality available from a keyboard. Give users enough time. Do not use content that causes seizures. Help users navigate and find content. | Can it function without confusion and without use of a mouse? |
| **U — Understandable** | Make text readable and understandable. Make content appear and operate in predictable ways. Help users avoid and correct mistakes. | Can a user understand how the UI functions and the information on the site? |
| **R — Robust** | Maximize compatibility with current and future user tools including assistive technologies. | Can different assistive devices (screen readers) understand the website? |

> **COMMON ACCESSIBILITY ISSUE:** 86.4% of home pages have **low contrast text** — the most common accessibility issue.

## Semantic HTML

- Screen readers get information about the **DOM (Document Object Model)** — the HTML structure of the page
- Screen readers will **NOT** read styles or JavaScript — only HTML structure
- By ensuring HTML is semantically correct, we provide clear information to the browser about the type of content and its relationships

## ARIA

ARIA (Assistive Rich Internet Applications) is a spec from W3C that provides extra information to screen readers via HTML attributes.

> **RULE: Use ARIA as a LAST RESORT!** Always prefer semantic HTML. ARIA adds information; semantic HTML provides it natively.

## Accessibility vs Usability vs Inclusive Design

| Concept | Definition & Scope |
|---|---|
| **Usability** | Whether designs are effective, efficient and satisfying to use. Theoretically includes accessibility but in practice does not specifically focus on people with disabilities. |
| **Accessibility** | Specifically focused on enabling people **WITH DISABILITIES** to use a product. Narrower scope than inclusive design. |
| **Inclusive Design** | Extends **BEYOND** accessibility. Aims to create products usable by the **WIDEST range of users** — regardless of gender, location, native language, and physical abilities. |

# Section 6: Information Architecture, Wireframing & Prototyping

## Information Architecture (IA)

> **DEFINITION:** IA is the discipline of making information **findable and understandable**. It includes searching, browsing, categorizing and presenting relevant and contextual information to help people understand their surroundings and find what they are looking for.

Designers need to understand:
- The **information needs** of users
- The **site or app's content**
- The **business goals** of the website, app, or organisation

## The IA Triad — Three Things That Inform Every IA Decision

| Element | Definition & Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Content** | The document or data types, content objects, volume, and existing structures. What kind of information is available? What relevance does it have to the user? |
| **Context** | Business goals, funding, culture, technology, politics, resources, and constraints. Where is the user seeking out the content? When, why, and how is the user engaging with the content? |
| **Users** | The audience, tasks, needs, experiences and how they seek information. Who is consuming the content? What value does it provide? What pre-existing expectations do they have? |

## Wireframes

A wireframe is a **schematic layout or blueprint** useful for designing a screen. Used to communicate about the **structure** of the software or website to programmers or cross-functional team members.

**How to create good wireframes:**
- **Clarity of goals:** What is that site page? What can the user do there?
- **Ensure user confidence:** Unpredictable pages or unexpected button placement diminishes confidence.
- **Keep it simple:** No real colours, fonts, images or final copy — just boxes and labels. Focus on structure and function, not aesthetics.

## Experiment & Prototyping

- **Experiment:** Testing a hypothesis in order to accept or reject it based on measurable results. Conducted for a finite amount of time.
- **Prototype:** A proof of concept that allows you to test before fully building the solution. Does not necessarily need to be animated or interactive.
- **Rapid prototyping:** A quick and cost-effective way to build and test a working version.

### Prototype Validation — 3 Questions Your Prototype Must Answer
1. Does the solution solve the problem?
2. Does the target customer find the key features valuable?
3. Would the target customer pay for the product or use it in a way that can be monetized?

### 4 Steps to Rapidly Prototyping a Value Proposition
1. Write a prototype outline — include all customer functional requirements and analysis
2. Get started with your prototype tool and organise your resources
3. Build out all screens of your prototype — add all necessary details, include screen size and responsiveness
4. Make it flow — show the result of an action or key feature, either through animation or before/after states

## MVP Types — Experimental Prototypes

| MVP Type | Definition, How it works & Example |
|---|---|
| **MVP (Min. Viable Product)** | A version of a product with just enough features to be usable by early customers who can then provide feedback for future development. Reduces unnecessary and lengthy work. |
| **Concierge MVP** | Attempts to simulate an aspect of the customer's experience WITHOUT the interface. Customer **KNOWS** a human is doing it manually. Example: 'Book a Maid' app — founders manually found maids and confirmed appointments. |
| **Wizard of Oz** | Participants interact with a product they believe to be FULLY FUNCTIONAL, but in reality it is controlled by an unseen human operator. Customer does **NOT** know a human is in the loop. Example: A Q&A service manually routed questions to experts, looked fully automated. |
| **Smoke Test** | Determines if there is sufficient customer demand. Does NOT simulate the full experience. Gauges interest by measuring button clicks or sign-ups WITHOUT having functionality. Tests: 'Can you break the design?' |
| **Explainer Video** | A short video or animation that explains the benefits of the product or how to navigate the application. Used to validate interest and communicate value before building. |
| **Mechanical Turk** | Building a frontend with a human-powered backend to manually simulate a complex digital product. Like a crowdsourced version of Wizard of Oz. |

## Rapid Prototyping Tools
Adobe XD · Figma · Sketch · InVision · Balsamiq

# Section 7: Designing for Mobile Screens

## Why Mobile Matters
- Phones are now the **PRIMARY devices** to access the internet
- By 2025, **5,000,000,000** projected mobile internet users worldwide
- The primary way a user experiences your brand might be via mobile

> Simply making a compact version of your website is no longer the way design is done. **Mobile-first or responsive-design** approaches are adopted by companies who want to ensure digital products can scale for the future.

## Challenges & Opportunities of Mobile Design

| Challenge | What it means for design |
|---|---|
| **Tiny screen** | Limited space. No sidebars, long menus, large decorative images. Strip to essence, keep most important functions on the front screen. |
| **Variable screen widths** | Difficult to design for screens ranging from 360px to 640px wide. Scrolling down is acceptable; width issues are the priority. |
| **Touch screens** | Buttons/links must be large enough to touch. Minimum **48x48dp (9mm)** for Android, **44pt x44pt** for Apple. Space between targets is critical. |
| **Difficulty of typing** | Design paths so typing is unnecessary or very limited. Use: auto-completion, fingerprint/face recognition for login, prefill wherever possible. |
| **Challenging environments** | Users are outside, in bright sun, in dark, on transport. Design for 'fat fingers'. Make mistakes easily correctable. |
| **Location awareness** | Mobile devices know exactly where they are. Use geolocation data to serve context-specific information. |
| **Social influences & attention** | Design for distracted users: make task sequences easy, quick, and re-entrant. Make everything self-explanatory. |

## How to Approach Mobile Design — 5 Principles

| Principle | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Know your users** | Understand their needs, tasks, and contexts of use. Mobile users use phones all the time in many ways. |
| **Strip to essence** | Don't be afraid to take away all extra content. Relevant content should appear HIGH on the screen. |
| **Use device hardware** | Take advantage of features not available on desktop: camera, GPS, accelerometer, microphone. |
| **Linearize content** | Order the mobile site's content VERTICALLY so it reads well — one column, top to bottom. |
| **Optimize common interactions** | Eliminate typing. Use as few screen loads as possible. Reduce scrolling and sideways dragging. Make information reachable with fewer tasks. |

## Mobile Design Patterns

Vertical stack · Filmstrip · Touch tools · Bottom navigation · Collections and cards · Infinite list · Generous borders · Loading or progress indicators · Richly connected apps

## Responsive Design vs Mobile-First

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| **Responsive Design** | Technologies that ensure the application works seamlessly across various devices and screen sizes. Adapts layout and content based on viewport. |
| **Mobile-First** | Design for the smallest screen first, then scale up. Forces focus on what truly matters. |
| **SPA (Single-Page App)** | Loads once and updates dynamically without full page reloads. Feels fast and app-like. |
| **MPA (Multi-Page App)** | Traditional model — each action loads a new page. Simpler but slower. |

# Section 8: Psychology of UX Design

## Core Idea

> UX design is not just designing interfaces. It is designing for the **LIMITATIONS** and **SHORTCUTS** of the human mind.

> Cognitive biases are NOT flaws in people. They are **coping mechanisms** for dealing with too much information, too little time, incomplete meaning, and limited memory.

## Why Cognitive Biases Exist

| Problem | How the brain responds |
|---|---|
| Too much information | We cannot pay attention to everything — the brain filters and focuses on what seems new, relevant, or unusual. |
| Not enough meaning | We fill gaps with assumptions — the brain completes patterns based on experience and expectations. |
| Need to act fast | We use shortcuts to decide quickly — the brain simplifies decisions to avoid paralysis. |
| Limited memory | We simplify what we remember — the brain edits and reconstructs details rather than perfectly recalling them. |

## Cognitive Biases

### Information Biases
- **Cognitive Load:** The total mental effort being used in working memory at a given moment. High cognitive load leads to errors, frustration, and abandonment. Design goal: **minimise cognitive load**.
- **Banner Blindness:** Users automatically ignore banner-shaped content — even when it contains important information. Place key info where users actually look.
- **Decoy Effect:** Adding a third, inferior option changes user preference between the original two options. Used in pricing to make a 'target' option seem better value.

### Meaning Biases
- **Aha! Moment:** The moment a user first genuinely realises the value of a product. Users who reach this moment are far more likely to continue using the product. Essential for retention.
- **Familiarity Bias:** Users prefer products that operate in ways similar to products they already know. Radical new interfaces face resistance even if objectively better.
- **Variable Reward:** Unpredictable rewards (like social media feeds) are more engaging than predictable ones. Can be used ethically or manipulatively.

### Time / Act Fast Biases
- **Loss Aversion:** The psychological pain of losing something is **twice as powerful** as the joy of gaining the same thing. "Don't miss out" framing is more powerful than "Get this benefit."
- **Hick's Law:** Increasing the number of choices will increase the time it takes to make a decision. More options = slower decisions. Reduce options = faster decisions = higher conversion.
- **Investment Loops:** Users who have invested time, data, or money in a product are less likely to leave — the more invested, the harder to quit.

### Memory Biases
- **Sensory Appeal:** Designs that engage multiple senses are more memorable and enjoyable.
- **Endowment Effect:** People value things more highly once they own them. Letting users 'own' or personalise something increases their attachment.
- **Storytelling Effect:** Information presented in the form of a story is more memorable than raw data. Narrative structure aids recall and emotional connection.

## Ethical vs Manipulative Design

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Helpful / Ethical Design** | Respects transparency, user choice, and the user's best interest. Guides users to good decisions honestly. Uses psychology to HELP, not exploit. |
| **Manipulative Design / Dark Patterns** | Exploits cognitive biases AGAINST users for product or business gain. Examples: Fake urgency ('Only 2 left!'), Social pressure ('1000 people viewing this'), FOMO, hidden unsubscribe links. |

> **Case study question format:** Which psychological principles are used? What UX problem is being solved? Is the design ethical or manipulative? Would you improve anything?

# Section 9: Choosing a Frontend Technology

## Factors to Consider

| Factor | Key considerations |
|---|---|
| **Project Requirements** | Is it a SPA, MPA, or real-time app (chat, live scores)? What features are needed? |

## Technology-Specific Notes
- **React:** Component-based architecture. Great for dynamic and interactive UIs. Vast ecosystem and strong community.
- **Vue.js:** Gentle learning curve. Balance between full-fledged framework and library. Highly customizable with reactive and composable data model.
- **Angular:** Full-fledged MVC framework. Well-suited for enterprise-level applications. Stronger opinions on how to structure applications.

## Decision-Making Process
1. **List down requirements** — clearly outline must-have features and performance expectations
2. **Research** — look into each technology's strengths and weaknesses relative to your requirements
3. **Prototype** — create small prototypes to evaluate each technology in practice
4. **Evaluate** — consider learning curve, development speed, and how well each option meets your UI/UX expectations

## Design & Interaction Principles

**Q: What is Don Norman's General Design Principle?**
> Good design is a lot harder to notice than poor design. Good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible. Bad design, on the other hand, screams.

**Q: What are the 5 steps of the Design Process?**
> 1. Design Brief, 2. Design Principles, 3. Sketching, 4. Sketchboards, 5. Task Flows / Wireframes.

**Q: What is the difference between an Affordance and a Signifier?**
> Affordance is the possible interaction between a person and an object — what CAN be done. Signifier is a visible signal communicating WHERE and HOW to perform that action. Signifiers are MORE IMPORTANT than affordances in design because they communicate how to use the design.

**Q: What are the 4 types of Constraints?**
> Physical (size/shape prevents wrong action), Cultural (social conventions like red = danger), Semantic (meaning limits options e.g. windshield in front of rider), Logical (only one sensible option remains).

**Q: What is Feedback and why must it be immediate?**
> Feedback communicates the results of a user's action. It must be immediate — even a delay of a tenth of a second is disconcerting. Long delays cause users to give up and go elsewhere.

**Q: What is a Conceptual Model?**
> A simplified explanation of how something works — does not have to be complete or accurate, just useful. Example: the file/folder icon model on a computer represents a physical filing cabinet.

**Q: What is Natural Mapping?**
> When the relationship between a control and its result follows physical or logical intuition — like a steering wheel turning the car in the direction you turn it. Best mappings need no labels.

## HCD

**Q: What is HCD and what problem does it solve?**
> Human-Centred Design puts human needs, capabilities, and behaviour FIRST then designs around them. It solves the problem of technology becoming too complex, where humans are blamed for 'errors' that are actually caused by poor design.

**Q: What is the most important rule of HCD?**
> Never solve the problem you are asked to solve. The secret to success is to understand what the REAL problem is.

**Q: What are the 4 stages of the HCD iterative cycle?**
> 1. Observation (Empathy & Define), 2. Idea Generation (Ideate), 3. Prototyping, 4. Testing. These repeat iteratively.

**Q: What are the three rules of Idea Generation in HCD?**
> 1. Generate numerous ideas — do not fixate on 1-2 early. 2. Be creative without constraints — avoid criticising ideas. 3. Question everything — what seems obvious might not be.

**Q: What is the difference between Activity-Centred and Human-Centred design?**
> Activity-centred focuses on the task (e.g. 'go shopping'). Human-centred focuses on the PERSON doing the task — their emotions, frustrations, true goals, and context.

**Q: What does 'diverge then converge' mean in HCD?**
> First, expand the scope of the problem and generate many ideas (diverge). Then, narrow down to the best solution (converge). Do not jump to solutions before exploring the full problem space.

## UX Research

**Q: What is the UX Research Process (5 steps)?**
> 1. Define (understand the problem), 2. Plan (choose research method), 3. Conduct (gather insights), 4. Analyze (synthesize findings), 5. Apply (implement into design decisions).

**Q: What is the difference between Quantitative and Qualitative research?**
> Quantitative = WHAT happens — numbers, statistics, analytics (surveys, Google Analytics). Qualitative = WHY it happens — emotions, motivations, depth (interviews, field studies, focus groups).

**Q: What is the difference between Attitudinal and Behavioral research?**
> Attitudinal = what users SAY (opinions, perceptions, attitudes). Behavioral = what users DO (observed actions). Behavioral is more reliable because actions don't lie. Always trust behavioral data more.

**Q: What is the difference between Analysis and Synthesis?**
> Analysis breaks research into facts. Synthesis brings all findings TOGETHER into insights, conclusions, and actionable recommendations — making facts meaningful.

**Q: What are the 4 steps of Affinity Mapping?**
> 1. Write all data points on post-its. 2. Stick all post-its on a wall/whiteboard. 3. Move post-its into groups by common themes. 4. Label each group to name the theme.

**Q: What is an Empathy Map?**
> A tool that categorises user knowledge into: Says, Thinks, Does, and Feels. It illustrates user attitudes and behaviours quickly and acts as a source of truth throughout a project.

**Q: What are the 4 GDPR requirements in UX Research?**
> 1. Informed Consent, 2. Data Minimization, 3. Right to Access and Erasure, 4. Data Security.

**Q: Name 3 types of bias in UX Research.**
> Sampling Bias (non-representative participants), Confirmation Bias (favouring info that confirms preconceptions), Social Desirability Bias (participants answer in socially acceptable ways).

## Accessibility

**Q: What does POUR stand for in WCAG?**
> Perceivable (info presentable to senses), Operable (navigable by all inputs), Understandable (clear and predictable), Robust (compatible with assistive technologies).

**Q: What are the three compliance levels of WCAG?**
> A (lowest acceptable), AA (standard — most organisations target this), AAA (most strict, hardest to achieve).

**Q: What is the most common accessibility issue on the web?**
> Low contrast text — 86.4% of home pages have text that falls below WCAG 2 AA contrast thresholds.

**Q: When should you use ARIA?**
> ARIA should be used as a LAST RESORT. Always prefer semantic HTML first. Screen readers work with proper HTML natively — ARIA only adds extra context for dynamic content.

**Q: What is the difference between Accessibility and Inclusive Design?**
> Accessibility focuses specifically on people with disabilities. Inclusive Design is broader — designing for the widest range of users regardless of gender, location, language, physical ability, or background.

## IA, Wireframing & Prototyping

**Q: What is the IA Triad?**
> Content (what information exists and its relevance), Context (business goals, funding, culture, constraints), Users (audience, tasks, needs, expectations). Every IA decision is informed by all three.

**Q: What is a Wireframe?**
> A schematic layout or blueprint for designing a screen — communicates structure and content placement without visual design details (no colours, fonts, or images).

**Q: What are the 3 questions a successful prototype must answer?**
> 1. Does it solve the problem? 2. Do target customers find key features valuable? 3. Would they pay for it or use it in a monetizable way?

**Q: What is the difference between Concierge and Wizard of Oz MVPs?**
> Concierge: customers KNOW a human is doing the work manually — it simulates the experience without an interface. Wizard of Oz: customers think the product is FULLY AUTOMATED but a human is secretly operating it behind the scenes.

**Q: What is a Smoke Test?**
> An MVP type that gauges customer demand by measuring actions (e.g. button clicks or sign-ups) WITHOUT actually having the backend or full functionality. Tests interest before building.

## Mobile & Psychology

**Q: What is the minimum touch target size for mobile?**
> 48x48dp (9mm) for Android devices and 44pt x44pt for Apple iOS. Space between targets is also critical.

**Q: What are the 5 principles for approaching mobile design?**
> 1. Know your users, 2. Strip to essence, 3. Use device hardware, 4. Linearize content, 5. Optimize common interactions.

**Q: What is Hick's Law?**
> The more choices you give a user, the longer it takes them to make a decision. Reduce options to speed up decision-making and prevent choice paralysis.

**Q: What is Loss Aversion?**
> The pain of losing something is twice as powerful as the joy of gaining the same thing. Used in UX copy: framing things as 'avoiding loss' is more powerful than 'gaining benefit'.

**Q: What is Banner Blindness?**
> The tendency for users to automatically ignore banner-shaped content — even when it contains important information. Key content must not be placed in banner-like areas.

**Q: What is the Decoy Effect?**
> Adding a third, inferior option changes user preference between the original two. Commonly used in pricing to make a 'target' option seem better value.

**Q: What is the ethical vs manipulative design distinction?**
> Ethical design respects transparency, user choice, and best interest. Manipulative design (dark patterns) exploits cognitive biases against users for product gain — fake urgency, FOMO, social pressure, hidden unsubscribe links.
